<br /><h3>🧠 1. The Alzheimer’s Breakthrough: p-tau217 and Triage</h3>

  <p>
    For decades, diagnosing Alzheimer’s required expensive PET scans or painful spinal taps. Today, Blood-Based
    Biomarkers (BBMs) are the new gold standard for triage.
  </p>
  <p></p>
  <ul>
    <li>How it works: Tests like p-tau217 measure specific proteins associated with brain plaques.</li>
    <li>
      The Triage Pathway: These tests allow primary care doctors to identify at-risk patients immediately, facilitating
      faster access to Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs).
    </li>
    <li>
      Key Advantage: It is a cost-effective alternative that reduces the 2-year wait times often seen for specialized
      neurology scans.
    </li>
  </ul>
  <p></p>

<h3>🫀 2. Predicting Heart Disease in Women: The 30-Year Window</h3>

  <p>
    Recent <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/posts/what-new-studies-show-about-slowing/" rel="dofollow" title="What New Studies Show About Slowing"><b>longitudinal studies show</b></a> that three specific markers can predict a woman's cardiovascular risk up to
    <strong>30 years in advance</strong>
    .
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Lipoprotein (a) [Lp(a)]:</strong>
        A "sticky" genetic particle. Unlike LDL, your levels are determined at birth and don't change with diet. Knowing
        this number early is vital for
        <strong>
          <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/posts/apob-analysis-heart-health-longevity/" target="_blank">
            preventing premature heart attacks
          </a>
        </strong>
        .
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>hs-CRP:</strong>
        This measures "hidden" inflammation. Even with low cholesterol, high CRP indicates your arteries are under
        constant fire.
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>ApoB:</strong>
        Increasingly viewed as more accurate than standard LDL-C for assessing the total number of atherogenic
        particles.
      </p>
    </li>
  </ul>

<h3>🎗️ 3. Multi-Cancer Early Detection (MCED) &amp; Proteomics</h3>

  <p>
    Standard blood work (CBC) often misses early-stage solid tumors. However,
    <strong>Liquid Biopsies</strong>
    are changing the landscape:
  </p>
  <ol>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Galleri Test:</strong>
        Uses DNA sequencing to identify signals for over 50 types of cancer by looking at fragments of tumor DNA (cfDNA)
        floating in the blood.
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Proteomics:</strong>
        A newer field that analyzes protein patterns. <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/posts/new-aging-research-march-2024/" rel="dofollow" title="New Aging Research March 2024"><b>New research suggests</b></a> these tests can detect many
        <strong>Stage I cancers</strong>
        that imaging would normally miss.
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Tumor Markers:</strong>
        While not always diagnostic, monitoring markers like
        <strong>PSA (Prostate)</strong>
        ,
        <strong>CA-125 (Ovarian)</strong>
        , or
        <strong>CEA (Colorectal)</strong>
        helps track if a treatment is working.
      </p>
    </li>
  </ol>

<h3>🧪 4. Beyond the Basics: Longevity Monitoring</h3>

  <p>For seniors, the most important blood tests are those that monitor the "filters" of the body:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Liver Function (LFTs):</strong>
        Monitoring ALT and AST isn't just for alcoholics anymore; it’s the first line of defense against
        <strong>NAFLD (Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease)</strong>
        .
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Kidney Health (eGFR):</strong>
        Since kidney decline is silent, tracking the Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate is essential for anyone on
        long-term medications or managing hypertension.
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Hormonal Balance:</strong>
        Evaluating
        <strong>Thyroid (TSH/T4)</strong>
        and
        <strong>DHEA</strong>
        levels can explain sudden fatigue, weight gain, or cognitive "fog" that is often misattributed to "just getting
        older."
      </p>
    </li>
  </ul>

<h3>❓ FAQ:&nbsp;</h3>

  <p><em>Targeting the specific questions Google's "People Also Ask" feature looks for.</em></p>
  <p>
    <strong>Q: Can a standard blood test show Alzheimer’s risk?</strong>
    <strong>A:</strong>
    Not a "standard" CBC. You must specifically request a
    <strong>p-tau217</strong>
    or
    <strong>amyloid-beta 42/40 ratio</strong>
    test. These are now available through major labs but are often listed as "investigational" or "premium" diagnostics.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Q: Why is my Lp(a) high if my LDL is low?</strong>
    <strong>A:</strong>
    Lp(a) is purely genetic. It is not influenced by statins, exercise, or keto diets. If your Lp(a) is high, you are at
    a higher risk for <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/posts/in-the-blood/" rel="dofollow" title="In The Blood"><b>blood clots and arterial narrowing regardless</b></a> of your "standard" cholesterol score.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Q: How often should a senior have liver function tests?</strong>
    <strong>A:</strong>
    If you are <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/posts/taking-vitamins-daily-isnt-best-for/" rel="dofollow" title="Taking Vitamins Daily Isn't Best for Health"><b>taking more than three daily</b></a> medications (polypharmacy), LFTs should be checked at least
    <strong>every 6 months</strong>
    to ensure your liver is processing those drugs without cellular damage.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Q: Can a blood test detect cancer even if I have no symptoms?</strong>
    <strong>A:</strong>
    Yes, through
    <strong>MCED (Multi-Cancer Early Detection)</strong>
    tests. However, these are currently used as a supplement to, not a replacement for, colonoscopies and mammograms.
  </p>

<h3>📌 Key Points for Your Next Lab Visit</h3>

  <ul>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Fasting Matters:</strong>
        Ensure 9–12 hours of fasting for accurate triglycerides and glucose, but
        <strong>hs-CRP</strong>
        and
        <strong>Lp(a)</strong>
        can be tested at any time.
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Request Your Raw Data:</strong>
        Don't just settle for "Your labs look normal." Ask for the numerical values of your
        <strong>A1c, eGFR, and Vitamin D3</strong>
        .
      </p>
    </li>
    <li>
      <p>
        <strong>Monitor Trends:</strong>
        A "normal" result that is slowly moving toward the edge of the range over three years is more important than a
        single one-time snapshot.
      </p>
    </li>
