<p>The <strong><a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2023/10/the-top-ten-growing-lifestyle-trends-in.html" target="_blank">WHO defines intrinsic capacity (IC)</a></strong> as <em>the composite of all the physical and mental capacities that an individual can draw on at any point in time</em>. Rather than focusing on diseases (e.g., arthritis, diabetes, heart disease), this framework focuses on <strong>what a person can actually do</strong>—functionally and cognitively—as they age (<a href="https://academic.oup.com/biomedgerontology/article/77/1/94/6338147?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">academic.oup.com</a>).</p>
<p>This marks a <strong>fundamental shift</strong> from disease‑centered care to <strong>function‑centered care</strong>, recognizing that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Two people with the same diagnosis can function very differently</li>
<li>Functional decline often begins <strong>before</strong> diagnosable disease</li>
<li>Preserving capacity can delay disability, dependence, and institutional care</li>
</ul>
<hr />
<h2><strong>Intrinsic Capacity vs. Functional Ability (Critical Distinction)</strong></h2>
<p>WHO distinguishes two closely related concepts:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Intrinsic Capacity:</strong> What your body and brain are capable of</li>
<li><strong>Functional Ability:</strong> What you are able to do <em>in your real environment</em></li>
</ul>
<p>Functional ability is shaped by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Intrinsic capacity</li>
<li>The physical environment (housing, walkability, assistive devices)</li>
<li>The social environment (support, access to care, community)</li>
</ul>
<p>This interaction is central to WHO’s <strong>Healthy Ageing model</strong> and underpins global aging policy from 2021–2030 (<a href="https://academic.oup.com/biomedgerontology/article/77/1/94/6338147?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">academic.oup.com</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h2><strong>The Five Domains of Intrinsic Capacity</strong></h2>
<p>WHO operationalizes intrinsic capacity into <strong>five measurable domains</strong>, now widely used in research and clinical care.</p>
<h3><strong>1. Locomotor Capacity (Movement &amp; Strength)</strong></h3>
<p>Includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Muscle strength</li>
<li>Balance</li>
<li>Gait speed</li>
<li>Ability to rise from a chair or climb stairs</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Why it matters:</strong><br />
Declines in locomotor capacity are among the strongest predictors of falls, disability, hospitalization, and loss of independence (<a href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35395736/?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h3><strong>2. Vitality (Energy &amp; Physiologic Reserves)</strong></h3>
<p>Includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Nutrition status</li>
<li>Metabolic health</li>
<li>Muscle mass</li>
<li>Fatigue resistance</li>
</ul>
<p>WHO and gerontology researchers increasingly emphasize nutrition as central to vitality—not a separate domain—because it underpins all other capacities (<a href="https://academic.oup.com/biomedgerontology/article-abstract/77/1/91/6499893?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">academic.oup.com</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h3><strong>3. Cognitive Capacity</strong></h3>
<p>Includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Memory</li>
<li>Attention</li>
<li>Executive function (planning, decision‑making)</li>
</ul>
<p>Cognitive decline can occur even in the absence of dementia and often predicts later functional impairment (<a href="https://bmcgeriatr.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12877-025-05709-y?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">bmcgeriatr.biomedcentral.com</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h3><strong>4. Psychological Capacity</strong></h3>
<p>Includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Emotional regulation</li>
<li>Mood stability</li>
<li>Stress resilience</li>
</ul>
<p>Depression and anxiety significantly accelerate decline across other IC domains, yet are often under‑recognized in older adults (<a href="https://bmcgeriatr.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12877-023-04362-7?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">bmcgeriatr.biomedcentral.com</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h3><strong>5. Sensory Capacity (Vision &amp; Hearing)</strong></h3>
<p>Includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Visual acuity</li>
<li>Hearing function</li>
</ul>
<p>Sensory loss is not benign—it is associated with cognitive decline, falls, social isolation, and <a href="https://www.aginghealth.website/2024/04/psoriasis-metabolic-syndrome-mortality-risk.html" rel="dofollow" title="Psoriasis Metabolic Syndrome Mortality Risk"><b>increased mortality risk when untreated</b></a> (<a href="https://bmcgeriatr.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12877-025-05709-y?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">bmcgeriatr.biomedcentral.com</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h2><strong>Why WHO Created the Intrinsic Capacity Framework</strong></h2>
<p>WHO introduced intrinsic capacity in its <em>World Report on Ageing and Health</em> to address major gaps in traditional healthcare:</p>
<h3><strong>Limitations of Traditional Models</strong></h3>
<ul>
<li>Over‑reliance on diagnoses</li>
<li>Age‑based clinical decisions</li>
<li>Fragmented specialty care</li>
<li>Late intervention after disability emerges</li>
</ul>
<h3><strong>Advantages of the IC Model</strong></h3>
<ul>
<li>Detects decline <strong>earlier</strong></li>
<li>Supports personalized care planning</li>
<li>Aligns medical, social, and environmental interventions</li>
<li>Reduces ageism by focusing on abilities, not age</li>
</ul>
<p>This paradigm is now foundational to WHO’s <strong>Integrated Care for Older People (ICOPE)</strong> program used globally (<a href="https://www.who.int/publications-detail-redirect/WHO-FWC-ALC-17.2?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">who.int</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h2><strong>How Intrinsic Capacity Is Measured in Practice</strong></h2>
<h3><strong>WHO ICOPE Two‑Step Assessment</strong></h3>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Brief Screening</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Quick, low‑resource screening across all five domains</li>
<li>High sensitivity (≈95%) for detecting decline</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Comprehensive Assessment</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Detailed evaluation of impaired domains</li>
<li>Guides targeted interventions</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<p>Large community studies confirm that IC screening predicts:</p>
<ul>
<li>Activities of daily living (ADLs)</li>
<li>Self‑care capacity</li>
<li>Social engagement</li>
<li>Future disability risk (<a href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35395736/?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov</a>).</li>
</ul>
<hr />
<h2><strong>Why Intrinsic Capacity Matters for Patients</strong></h2>
<p>From a patient empowerment perspective, intrinsic capacity:</p>
<ul>
<li>Helps you <strong>track meaningful health changes</strong></li>
<li>Provides language to discuss function—not just diagnoses—with clinicians</li>
<li>Supports shared decision‑making</li>
<li>Encourages earlier, lower‑risk interventions (exercise, nutrition, vision correction, mental health support)</li>
</ul>
<p>Importantly, research shows that <strong>each IC domain independently predicts adverse outcomes</strong>, meaning small, early declines are clinically meaningful (<a href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34343305/?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov</a>).</p>
<hr />
<h2><strong>Clinical and Policy Implications</strong></h2>
<p>Intrinsic capacity is now used to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Design age‑friendly communities</li>
<li>Guide preventive care</li>
<li>Prioritize non‑pharmacologic interventions</li>
<li>Inform global aging policy</li>
</ul>
<p>WHO emphasizes that improving IC across the lifespan—not just in old age—offers the greatest public health return (<a href="https://springerlink.fh-diploma.de/article/10.14283/jfa.2024.30?utm_source=openai" target="_blank">springerlink.fh-diploma.de</a>).</p>

<h2><strong>How Intrinsic Capacity (IC) Maps to Exercise and Therapy Decisions</strong></h2><h3><span style="font-size: normal; font-weight: normal;">Big Picture: How Clinicians Use IC</span></h3>

<p><span style="font-size: normal; font-weight: normal;">Instead of asking <em>“What disease do you have?”</em>, IC reframes the question as:</span></p>
<blockquote node="[object Object]">
<p><span style="font-size: normal; font-weight: normal;">“Which capacities are declining, and which interventions best restore or preserve them?”</span></p>
</blockquote>
<p><span style="font-size: normal; font-weight: normal;">This leads to:</span></p>
<ul>
<li><span style="font-size: normal; font-weight: normal;">More personalized therapy plans</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: normal; font-weight: normal;">Earlier, lower‑risk interventions</span></li>
<li><span style="font-size: normal; font-weight: normal;">Better functional outcomes than diagnosis‑only care</span></li>
</ul>

    <h3 class="faq-question">What are the key components of the Intrinsic Capacity Framework?</h3>
    <p class="faq-answer">The key components of the Intrinsic Capacity Framework include cognitive, psychological, and physical functions. It assesses an individual's ability to perform daily tasks, manage stress, and maintain social connections. The framework also considers environmental and social factors that influence an individual's capacity, such as access to healthcare, social support, and community resources. These components provide a <b>comprehensive understanding of an individual's overall capacity</b>.</p>
