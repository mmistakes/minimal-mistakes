<h2>The Connection: Liver, Heart, and Brain</h2>
<div class="clinical-fact-box">
  <p>You might wonder what your liver has to do with stroke risk. The answer lies in your "internal plumbing."</p>
  <ul>
    <li><b>Liver Pressure:</b> When cirrhosis scars the liver, blood pressure builds in the portal vein.</li>
    <li><b>The Heart's Role:</b> Medications like Carvedilol are prescribed to "un-kink the hose" by relaxing liver vessels and calming the heart's output.</li>
    <li><b>The Brain's Safety:</b> If systemic blood pressure is unstable due to liver complications, the delicate vessels in the brain are at higher risk for the blockages that cause TIAs.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="faq-section">
  <h2>5-Question Clinical FAQ for Aging Health</h2>
  
  <h4>1. Can Carvedilol lower the risk of a mini stroke (TIA)?</h4>
  <p>While Carvedilol is primarily prescribed for heart failure or portal hypertension, it protects the brain indirectly by stabilizing systemic blood pressure and reducing the "pounding" effect of high pressure on cerebral vessels.</p>

  <h4>2. Why is the first 48 hours after mini-stroke symptoms so critical?</h4>
  <p>A TIA is often caused by a temporary blockage. However, the underlying cause remains, and the risk of a full-scale stroke is statistically highest within this immediate 48-hour window.</p>

  <h4>3. Does liver cirrhosis increase the complexity of stroke recovery?</h4>
  <p>Yes. The liver produces clotting factors. Managing a TIA in cirrhosis patients requires a "Vascular Bridge" approach where liver pressure and brain circulation are monitored simultaneously.</p>

  <h4>4. What does "Brain Fog" have to do with Portal Hypertension?</h4>
  <p>High portal pressure can lead to Hepatic Encephalopathy. This causes "brain fog" that can mimic TIA symptoms, making clinical evaluation essential to distinguish a metabolic issue from a vascular one.</p>

  <h4>5. How should I track my "Mental Clarity" score?</h4>
  <p>Using a <b>Daily Glucose Tracker</b> or a mental clarity log helps identify fluctuations. Presenting this data to your medical team allows for more precise treatment adjustments.</p>
