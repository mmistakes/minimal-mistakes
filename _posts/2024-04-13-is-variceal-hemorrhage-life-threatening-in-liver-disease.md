My wife took me to the hospital emergency room in a nearby town where they discovered I had a ruptured varices bleeding profusely. I was then transported by ambulance to Springfield, Missouri, for surgery where they banded the varices. The cause?&nbsp;<a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/dangers-of-excess-ammonia-in-blood/">My liver cirrhosis</a>. Will it improve? No, according to my gastroenterologist, <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/liver-cirrhosis-varices-banding-recovery/">even with a new liver, the damage is irreversible</a>.</p><table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="tr-caption-container" style="float: left;"><tbody><tr><td style="text-align: center;"><a href="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEhPBXjE2xp0WjLq1R2-R_bN5DXGz5BV0YIu3O5zrMK2eIkXISANFmTYWUA3KcWMOo6_VyHSZCBjIx9TfoM9I3ciayZS4Cm4JwWZM2kFTqpk610TCwycs6fDjjJ2c_knkFr6BdpSysc1hXjH-V9USUxYlQVo3UfB5L61T6if6MBLzWQ7VqOdXZNoF93ebNr_/s1025/The%20image%20shows%20a%20cross-section%20of%20the%20human%20body,%20with%20the%20liver%20and%20esophagus%20highlighted..webp" style="clear: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: auto; margin-right: auto;"><img alt="Cirrhosis and Esophageal Varices: This AI-generated image shows a cross-section of the human body, with the liver and esophagus highlighted." border="0" data-original-height="586" data-original-width="1025" src="https://blogger.googleusercontent.com/img/b/R29vZ2xl/AVvXsEhPBXjE2xp0WjLq1R2-R_bN5DXGz5BV0YIu3O5zrMK2eIkXISANFmTYWUA3KcWMOo6_VyHSZCBjIx9TfoM9I3ciayZS4Cm4JwWZM2kFTqpk610TCwycs6fDjjJ2c_knkFr6BdpSysc1hXjH-V9USUxYlQVo3UfB5L61T6if6MBLzWQ7VqOdXZNoF93ebNr_/s16000/The%20image%20shows%20a%20cross-section%20of%20the%20human%20body,%20with%20the%20liver%20and%20esophagus%20highlighted..webp" title="Cirrhosis and Esophageal Varices." /></a></td></tr><tr><td class="tr-caption" style="text-align: center;">Liver cirrhosis is a progressive disease where healthy liver tissue is replaced by scar tissue, impairing liver function. The liver is crucial in this system, tasked with detoxification, protein synthesis, and bile production. Chronic liver disease can disrupt this essential organ, leading to several complications, with variceal hemorrhage being among the most severe.</div><div><br />This blog post delves into the world of variceal hemorrhage, explaining its causes, <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/anemia-what-you-need-to-know/">associated risks</a>, and potential dangers. We'll also explore treatment options to help individuals with liver disease understand this condition and navigate its management.<br /><br /><h2 style="text-align: left;">What is Variceal Hemorrhage?</h2>Variceal hemorrhage, commonly referred to as esophageal variceal bleeding, is a serious condition that arises when dilated veins within the esophagus, known as varices, burst and cause bleeding. The <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/blog-post/">esophagus</a> itself is a muscular tube that forms a critical part of the digestive tract, facilitating the passage of food from the throat to the stomach. Under normal circumstances, the veins in the esophagus are responsible for transporting blood from the digestive organs back to the liver for detoxification and processing.<br /><br /><div>In chronic liver conditions, especially cirrhosis, the normal blood flow through the liver is hindered. This obstruction causes a rise in pressure within the portal vein, which is the primary vessel transporting blood from the intestines and spleen to the liver. Consequently, the blood is forced to find alternate pathways, leading to the expansion and thinning of veins in the esophagus and stomach, known as varices.</div><div><br /></div><div>Varices are enlarged submucosal veins, typically found in the lower part of the esophagus or upper part of the stomach. They are linked to portal hypertension from various causes such as presinusoidal (portal vein thrombosis), sinusoidal (cirrhosis), and <a href="https://www.ajronline.org/doi/pdfplus/10.2214/AJR.12.9098#:~:text=Budd-Chiari%20syndrome%20%28BCS%29%20is%20an%20uncommon%20condition%20characterized,BCS%20is%20an%20example%20of%20postsinusoidal%20portal%20hypertension." target="_blank">postsinusoidal (Budd Chiari syndrome)</a>, with<a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/liver-cirrhosis-what-you-need-to-know/" target="_blank"> cirrhosis being the most common</a>. Despite improvements in treatment in recent years, variceal bleeding still carries a mortality rate of at least 20% within 6 weeks.</div><div><br /></div><div>These varices are highly susceptible to tearing, which can result in severe internal bleeding. A variceal hemorrhage constitutes a critical medical emergency that demands prompt intervention.</div><h2 style="text-align: left;">What causes a Variceal Hemorrhage?</h2>Variceal hemorrhage is commonly caused by portal hypertension, which is often a result of liver cirrhosis. The increased pressure in the portal vein leads to the development of enlarged, swollen veins, known as varices, in the esophagus or stomach that can rupture and bleed. Other causes include blood clots in the portal vein or surrounding veins and parasitic infections like <a href="https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/22631-schistosomiasis">schistosomiasis.</a></div><div><br /></div><div>Cirrhosis itself can be the endpoint of various <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/restless-leg-syndrome/">liver conditions</a>, such as:</div><div><p style="text-align: left;"></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Hepatitis B and C:</b> These viral infections lead to liver inflammation and subsequent organ damage.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Alcoholic Liver Disease </b>refers to liver damage caused by extended excessive <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/dry-january/">consumption of alcohol.</a></li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD):&nbsp;</b>Excessive fat accumulation in the liver characterizes this condition, which can progress to Nonalcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH), involving inflammation and more severe liver damage. This progression is what led to my liver cirrhosis.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)</b>: A chronic autoimmune disorder that progressively destroys the bile ducts within the liver.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC):</b> Like Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC), this condition is an autoimmune disorder that leads to inflammation and subsequent scarring of the bile ducts.</li></ul><p></p></div><div><h2 style="text-align: left;">Risk Factors for Variceal Hemorrhage</h2>While portal hypertension is the main culprit, certain factors increase the risk of variceal bleeding: <br /><p style="text-align: left;"></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Severity of Liver Disease:</b> Individuals with advanced stages of cirrhosis are at higher risk. </li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Size of Varices:</b> Larger varices are more susceptible to rupture. </li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Red Signs:</b> Vomiting bright red blood, black tarry stools, and feeling lightheaded or dizzy are warning signs of potential bleeding. </li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Certain Medications: </b><a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/watchman-device-prevent-clots/">Blood thinners and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)</a> can increase the risk of bleeding.</li></ul><h2>The Perils of Variceal Hemorrhage</h2>Variceal hemorrhage represents a severe medical emergency with potentially fatal outcomes. The severity of bleeding can span from minimal to catastrophic, potentially inducing shock. The dangers of variceal hemorrhage are manifold and include:<br /><p style="text-align: left;"></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Blood Loss: </b>Profound blood loss can precipitate <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/anemia-what-you-need-to-know/">anemia</a>, leading to profound fatigue and generalized weakness.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Shock:</b> Intense hemorrhaging may precipitate a precipitous <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/hematologists/">decline in blood</a> pressure, culminating in hypovolemic shock, a condition that can be lethal.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Infection:</b> The presence of blood within the digestive tract due to internal bleeding can escalate the risk of infections.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Re-bleeding: </b>There is a heightened risk of subsequent bleeding episodes for those who have suffered from variceal hemorrhage.</li></ul><p></p><h2 style="text-align: left;">The Symptoms of Variceal Hemorrhage</h2>While varices may not present symptoms, certain signs can indicate a variceal hemorrhage. These include vomiting bright red blood or blood clots, known as hematemesis, and the presence of black, tarry stools, referred to as melena. Symptoms such as lightheadedness, dizziness, or weakness may occur due to the blood loss.&nbsp;</div><div><br /></div><div>Additionally, one might experience <a href="https://www.randomthoughts.bio/posts/what-is-leaky-gut-syndrome-and-how-do/">abdominal pain</a> and a rapid heart rate as the body tries to compensate for the blood loss by delivering oxygen more efficiently. Decreased urine output is another sign, as it reflects reduced blood flow to the kidneys, resulting in less urine production. In severe cases, the blood loss can impair brain function, causing confusion or disorientation.<br /><h2 style="text-align: left;">Diagnosis of Variceal Hemorrhage</h2><div>Prompt diagnosis of variceal hemorrhage is crucial for its effective management and treatment. Physicians consider the patient's medical history and symptoms and conduct a comprehensive physical examination. Commonly used diagnostic methods include:</div><div><p style="text-align: left;"></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Endoscopy</b> involves the insertion of a slender, flexible tube equipped with a camera through the mouth or nose to inspect the esophagus and stomach for varices and bleeding sites.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>An Upper GI Series</b>, also known as a Barium Swallow, entails a series of X-rays taken after the patient ingests a barium-containing liquid. This substance coats the digestive tract, allowing for clear visualization of varices.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Blood tests</b> are conducted to evaluate blood cell counts and liver function and to detect any issues with blood clotting.</li></ul></div><h2 style="text-align: left;">Treatment Options for Variceal Hemorrhage</h2>The primary goals of variceal hemorrhage treatment are to stop the bleeding, prevent re-bleeding, and address the underlying liver disease. Here's an overview of treatment options:&nbsp;</div><h3 style="text-align: left;">Endoscopic Therapy: </h3><div>This minimally invasive approach uses many different techniques to stop bleeding and prevent future episodes. Techniques include: <br /><p style="text-align: left;"></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Band Ligation:</b> Small rubber bands are placed around the base of varices to cut off blood supply and promote clotting. This is the procedure used for my varices. I have had banding surgery 3 times doing five at a time.&nbsp;</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Sclerotherapy:</b> An injection of a sclerosing agent into the varices shrinks and closes the blood vessels.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Balloon Tamponade:</b> A special balloon catheter is inserted into the esophagus and inflated to apply pressure on bleeding varices. This is a temporary measure often used to stabilize the patient before definitive treatment.</li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS Procedure)</b>: This minimally invasive procedure creates a new connection between the portal vein and a hepatic vein, bypassing the blockage in the liver and reducing pressure on the varices.</li></ul><p></p></div><div><h3 style="text-align: left;">Medications:</h3><p style="text-align: left;"></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Vasopressin:</b> This is a medication that narrows blood vessels and aids in the management of bleeding. It is commonly used to increase blood pressure in adults with dangerously low levels, particularly when other treatments have failed. Additionally, vasopressin has applications beyond its primary use, including the treatment of diabetes insipidus and as part of therapy for gastrointestinal bleeding, ventricular tachycardia, and ventricular defibrillation. </li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Octreotide:</b> Octreotide is a medication that decreases blood flow to the portal vein, which results in reduced pressure and supports blood clotting processes.</li></ul><h3 style="text-align: left;">Liver Transplant: </h3></div><div><p>In cases of advanced liver disease, particularly when variceal hemorrhage poses a grave and imminent threat to one's life, the medical intervention of a liver transplant may be deemed necessary. This complex surgical procedure involves the replacement of a damaged liver with a healthy donor organ, to restore normal liver function and prevent further complications.&nbsp;</p><p>Liver transplantation is considered a definitive treatment option for individuals with end-stage liver disease, providing them with a chance to significantly improve their quality of life and overall prognosis. The process of evaluating a patient for a liver transplant includes careful assessment of their medical history, current condition, and suitability for the surgery, followed by meticulous post-operative care to ensure a successful recovery and long-term outcome.</p><h2 style="text-align: left;">Liver Disease in the U.S.: Statistics and Trends</h2><p>More than 100 million individuals in the United States are affected by some form of liver disease. Specifically, 4.5 million adults in the U.S. have received a diagnosis of liver disease, representing 1.8% of the population. However, it is believed that between 80-100 million adults in the U.S. are living with fatty liver disease, with many being unaware of their condition. If left untreated, liver disease can progress to liver failure and liver cancer.&nbsp;</p><p>In 2020, 51,642 adults in the U.S. lost their lives due to liver disease, ranking it as the 12th leading cause of death. Chronic liver disease/cirrhosis was also identified as the 8th leading cause of death among non-Hispanic African American/Black individuals aged 45–64 years old. The primary causes of cirrhosis include hepatitis and other viruses, prolonged alcohol abuse, and nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).&nbsp;</p><p>Risk factors for liver disease encompass heavy alcohol consumption, obesity, type 2 diabetes, and exposure to specific chemicals or toxins. Cirrhosis heightens the risk of stroke, with elevated rates observed among Black/African American individuals.</p><h2 style="text-align: left;">What are the Benefits and Risks of Liver Transplantation for Older Patients?</h2><p><i>Liver transplantation</i> is a unique procedure that defies the higher risks typically associated with surgeries in individuals over the age of 65. The liver's remarkable ability to regenerate and replace its own cells, although severe liver disease may necessitate a transplant. Interestingly, the age demographic at highest risk for other major surgeries exhibits improved chances of survival following a liver transplant.&nbsp;</p><p>Each surgical procedure carries inherent risks, and the decision to proceed with a liver transplant involves a careful evaluation of the benefits versus the risks. The recipient's body must endure the removal of the diseased liver, the insertion of a donor's liver, the potential rejection of the new organ, and the subsequent recovery process. The demand for liver transplants surpasses the available organs, with 17,000 individuals awaiting a transplant in the United States.&nbsp;</p><p>While <i>older patients</i> have traditionally been viewed as high-risk candidates, studies indicate that their less aggressive immune systems contribute to more favorable surgical outcomes. The survival rates for elderly patients post-liver transplant are comparable to those of younger patients, boasting a 65% 3-year survival rate. It is crucial to remember that individual circumstances may differ, underscoring the importance of seeking personalized advice from healthcare professionals. On the whole, approximately 75% of individuals who undergo a liver transplant go on to live for at least five years</p><h2 style="text-align: left;">Living with Liver Disease and Risk of Variceal Hemorrhage</h2>Individuals with chronic liver disease require ongoing medical care to monitor their condition and manage the risk of variceal hemorrhage. Here are some essential steps: <br /><p style="text-align: left;"></p><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Follow doctor's recommendations: </b>This includes adhering to medication regimens, attending regular checkups, and following dietary restrictions to manage liver health. </li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Avoid risk factors: </b>This includes avoiding alcohol consumption, tobacco use, and certain medications that can worsen liver function. </li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Early detection:</b> Being aware of the warning signs of variceal hemorrhage and seeking immediate medical attention if any symptoms arise is crucial. </li></ul><ul style="text-align: left;"><li><b>Support Groups:</b> Connecting with others facing similar challenges can provide emotional support and valuable information.</li></ul><h4 style="text-align: left;">Conclusion</h4>Variceal hemorrhage is a serious complication of chronic liver disease. Early diagnosis and prompt treatment are essential to prevent life-threatening consequences. By understanding the causes, risks, and treatment options, individuals with liver disease can take proactive steps to manage their condition and minimize the risk of variceal bleeding.</div><h4 style="text-align: left;">Disclaimer:</h4><div>Although I am not a medical professional, I have experienced a variceal hemorrhage and suffer from liver cirrhosis. In the future, I may need to undergo a liver transplant. I hope the information in this blog post can prevent others from enduring what I have.</div><div><br /></div>
